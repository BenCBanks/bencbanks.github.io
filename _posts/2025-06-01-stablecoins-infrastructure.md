Most stablecoin debates are about the wrong thing.

The question is not whether USDC or USDT will replace the dollar. They will not. The question is whether stablecoin settlement rails will replace the correspondent banking network for cross-border payments — and there, the answer is more interesting.

Correspondent banking is slow, expensive, and opaque. A payment from the US to Southeast Asia can touch three or four intermediary banks, each taking a fee and adding a day. The total cost and delay is significant, and it falls disproportionately on smaller businesses and lower-income recipients who have no alternative.

Stablecoin settlement sidesteps the correspondent banking chain. If both endpoints can hold and move stablecoins — or if a payment processor handles the on/off ramp — the settlement layer becomes faster and cheaper. This is not speculative. It is already happening in remittance corridors where the banking infrastructure is weak.

The bet is not on a stablecoin becoming a reserve currency. The bet is on stablecoin infrastructure becoming the plumbing for a slice of cross-border commerce where the current plumbing is genuinely bad. That is a much more tractable problem, and it is already being solved.
